France vs Iraq: Forty Years in the Making, About to Meet Reality

Monday, June 22, 2026 · Lincoln Financial Field, Philadelphia

Forty years ago, Iraq played in their first and, until now, only World Cup. A generation of conflict, instability, and football rebuilt from almost nothing followed. That this team exists at all — never mind that it is walking out at Lincoln Financial Field to face the reigning runners-up — is one of the genuine human stories of this tournament. But stories don't slow down Kylian Mbappe, and Iraq are about to find that out in the most direct way possible.

France Arrive With Momentum and a Point to Prove

Didier Deschamps' side opened their campaign with a 3-1 win over Senegal, a result that papered over a few uncomfortable defensive moments but confirmed what most already suspected — this France attack, built around Mbappe and reigning Ballon d'Or winner Ousmane Dembele, is simply too sharp for most defences in this tournament to handle for ninety minutes.

Mbappe in particular arrives with extra motivation. He sits one goal short of Olivier Giroud's all-time France scoring record, and a fixture against a side ranked outside the world's top fifty is precisely the kind of match that record-chasing strikers circle on the calendar.

"This isn't really a question of if France win. It's a question of how many, and whether Iraq's resistance lasts the full ninety minutes."

What Iraq Can Realistically Defend

Iraq's route here was earned the hard way, through an inter-confederation playoff that demanded composure under genuine pressure. That same composure will be required in industrial quantities against a France front line that scored three against a well-organised Senegal side in their opener.

The realistic gameplan is damage limitation: a deep, disciplined block, minimal risk-taking in possession, and hoping that France's occasional defensive lapses — visible against Senegal — offer a sliver of a chance on the counter. Nobody inside Iraq's camp will be planning around winning this match. The planning is around walking away with dignity intact.

The Tactical Picture

Expect Deschamps to rotate slightly, with an eye on the more demanding fixture against Norway to come. Even a rotated France XI carries more individual quality than Iraq's entire matchday squad, and the gap in physicality and game intelligence at this level is likely to show early.
